### Title

### Overview
Ocean acidification is often called 'the other CO2 problem' â€” less famous than global warming but potentially just as devastating. The chemistry is straightforward, the evidence is irrefutable, and the consequences for marine life are already visible. Students investigate the driving question: How is the CO2 from your car slowly dissolving the shells of sea creatures thousands of miles away? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Trace the path of CO2 from emission to ocean absorption
- Model how dissolved CO2 changes ocean pH through chemical reactions
- Explain the relationship between ocean acidification and coral/shell health
- Predict the impact of different emission scenarios on marine ecosystems

### Component List (Starting Model: 4 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| CO2 Emissions | External | The rate at which humans release carbon dioxide into the atmosphere from burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and industry. |
| Ocean CO2 Absorption | Internal | How much atmospheric CO2 dissolves into seawater. The ocean absorbs about 30% of human CO2 emissions. |
| Ocean pH | Internal | The acidity level of seawater. As more CO2 dissolves, pH decreases (becomes more acidic). |
| Coral Health | Internal | The ability of coral reefs and shell-building organisms to survive. Lower pH dissolves their calcium carbonate structures. |

â€¢ The Chemistry of Ocean Acidification:
  When CO2 dissolves in seawater, it reacts with water to form carbonic acid: CO2 + H2O â†’ H2CO3. This carbonic acid then dissociates, releasing hydrogen ions (H+) that lower pH: H2CO3 â†’ H+ + HCO3-. The excess hydrogen ions also react with carbonate ions (CO3Â²-) that marine organisms need for building shells: H+ + CO3Â²- â†’ HCO3-. This is why acidification is a double hit â€” it increases acidity AND removes the building blocks organisms need.

â€¢ The Scale of the Problem:
  The ocean has absorbed approximately 525 billion tons of CO2 from the atmosphere since the Industrial Revolution â€” about 30% of all human emissions. Surface ocean pH has dropped from 8.2 to 8.1. While 0.1 sounds tiny, pH is logarithmic: this represents a 30% increase in hydrogen ion concentration. Current projections show pH could drop to 7.8 by 2100 â€” a 150% increase in acidity from pre-industrial levels.

â€¢ Biological Impacts:
  Organisms that build calcium carbonate structures â€” corals, oysters, clams, sea urchins, and many plankton species â€” are most vulnerable. Below a pH threshold, their shells and skeletons literally dissolve faster than they can build them. Coral reefs, which support 25% of all marine species despite covering less than 1% of the ocean floor, are facing dissolution. Pteropods (sea butterflies) at the base of many food chains are already showing shell thinning.

â€¢ The Recovery Problem:
  Even if CO2 emissions stopped completely today, ocean acidification would continue for decades as existing atmospheric CO2 continues dissolving. Full recovery of ocean pH would take tens of thousands of years because the deep ocean circulation that mixes surface and deep water operates on millennial timescales. This makes prevention far more effective than cure.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
â€¢ "The ocean is basic so it can handle some acid"
  Reality: While seawater is indeed slightly basic (pH 8.1), marine life evolved over millions of years in this exact pH range. A shift of 0.1 pH units represents a 30% change in acidity â€” equivalent to changing the oxygen concentration in air by 30%. Organisms can't just 'handle' that.
  Strategy: Analogy: Human blood pH is 7.4. A change of just 0.2 pH units (to 7.2) causes acidosis and can be fatal. Small pH changes matter enormously to biology.

â€¢ "CO2 is natural so it can't be harmful"
  Reality: CO2 is natural and essential for photosynthesis. The problem isn't CO2 existing â€” it's the RATE of increase. Natural CO2 cycles take thousands of years; we've increased atmospheric CO2 by 50% in 150 years. The ocean can absorb CO2 gradually, but not at this pace. It's like the difference between rain (natural) and a flood (too much, too fast).
  Strategy: Show the Keeling Curve: atmospheric CO2 was 280 ppm for 10,000 years, then shot to 420 ppm in 150 years. That rate is unprecedented.

â€¢ "We can just add chemicals to fix the ocean"
  Reality: The ocean contains 1.335 billion cubic kilometers of water. To raise its pH by 0.1 units would require billions of tons of alkaline minerals. We'd need to mine, transport, and distribute more material than the entire global mining industry produces. And we'd need to keep doing it every year. Prevention (cutting emissions) is millions of times more practical than cure.
  Strategy: Calculate: The ocean volume is 1.335 Ã— 10^18 cubic meters. How many tons of limestone would you need per cubic meter? Multiply out to see the absurdity.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: How is the CO2 from your car slowly dissolving the shells of sea creatures thousands of miles away?
Answer: CO2 from your car enters the atmosphere, and about 30% dissolves into the ocean. When CO2 meets seawater, it forms carbonic acid (H2CO3), which releases hydrogen ions that lower pH. This more acidic water dissolves the calcium carbonate that corals and shellfish need to build their structures. The chemistry is simple and inescapable: more CO2 in = more acid = less coral. The ocean has been absorbing our CO2 for decades â€” it's helped slow atmospheric warming but is paying with its own chemistry.

Reflection Exemplars:
â€¢ Q: Why is a 0.1 pH change significant?

â€¢ Q: Why can't the ocean just recover?

Even if we stopped adding CO2 today, it would take 10,000-50,000 years for ocean chemistry to return to pre-industrial levels.
