BACKGROUND CONTENT:
â€¢ From Food to Glucose:
  When you eat, digestion breaks complex food molecules into simpler ones. Carbohydrates are broken down into glucose (C6H12O6). This glucose enters the bloodstream and is delivered to every cell in your body. An average person's body contains about 4 grams of glucose in their blood at any time.

â€¢ Cellular Respiration:
  The equation: C6H12O6 + 6O2 â†’ 6CO2 + 6H2O + ~36 ATP. This happens in the mitochondria of every cell. The process has three stages: glycolysis (splitting glucose), the Krebs cycle (extracting electrons), and the electron transport chain (making most of the ATP).

â€¢ ATP: The Energy Currency:
  ATP is like cash for your cells. Every muscle contraction, nerve impulse, and chemical reaction is paid for with ATP. Your body produces and uses about 40 kg of ATP per day â€” roughly your body weight! But you only have about 250 grams at any given time, so it's constantly being recycled.

â€¢ Aerobic vs. Anaerobic:
  With oxygen (aerobic): 1 glucose â†’ ~36 ATP (efficient). Without enough oxygen (anaerobic): 1 glucose â†’ 2 ATP + lactic acid (inefficient). During intense exercise, muscles outpace oxygen delivery and switch to anaerobic respiration. The lactic acid buildup causes the burning feeling in your muscles.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
â€¢ "Food gives you energy directly"
  Reality: Food doesn't power muscles directly. Digestion breaks food into glucose, which cells convert to ATP through respiration. ATP is the actual energy molecule that powers all cellular work.
  Strategy: Analogy: You can't pour crude oil into a car engine. It must be refined into gasoline first. Similarly, food must be 'refined' into ATP.

â€¢ "You only breathe to get oxygen"
  Reality: You breathe for TWO reasons: to bring in O2 (input for respiration) AND to expel CO2 (waste product of respiration). Both are equally important. Too much CO2 is toxic.
  Strategy: Show the full respiration equation â€” both sides matter.

â€¢ "Energy is created when you eat"
  Reality: Energy is TRANSFORMED, not created. The chemical energy stored in food molecules (glucose) is converted to ATP energy through respiration. The total energy is conserved.
  Strategy: Connect to conservation of energy from physics.

â€¢ Maximum Effort Scenario: With high glucose and maximum oxygen, ATP Production is maximized. Athletic Output reaches peak levels. The model shows the body is efficiently converting food energy into motion through aerobic cellular respiration.
â€¢ No Fuel Scenario: Without food (glucose), ATP Production drops dramatically even if oxygen is plentiful. Athletic Output crashes. You can breathe all you want â€” without fuel, there's nothing to burn. This models what happens when an athlete 'hits the wall.'

Reflection Exemplars:
â€¢ Q: Why do marathon runners hit the wall?
  A: After about 90-120 minutes of intense exercise, the body depletes its stored glycogen (glucose reserves). Without glucose, ATP production drops dramatically. The runner suddenly feels exhausted, weak, and may have difficulty thinking clearly because the brain also needs glucose.
â€¢ Q: Why do you breathe harder during exercise?
  A: Exercise increases the demand for ATP, which increases the need for oxygen (a required input for efficient cellular respiration). Your brain detects rising CO2 levels (a waste product of respiration) and signals you to breathe faster and deeper to bring in more O2 and expel more CO2.
